The Story

Serra Luma means Mountain of Light.

Perched high above the Byron Bay Hinterland on Bundjalung Country, Serra Luma is a place of stillness, perspective and possibility.

More than a residence, it is a sanctuary woven into the landscape — a place where native forest, flowing water, sweeping views and architectural beauty combine to create an experience increasingly difficult to find in the modern world.

For some, Serra Luma will be a private family estate. For others, a creative refuge. For others still, the foundation for a retreat, wellness destination or regenerative legacy. Whatever its future chapter, Serra Luma remains a place designed to reconnect people with what matters most.

The Architecture

Architecture in Conversation with the Landscape

Serra Luma is the work of Philip Pratt, an architect celebrated for his Bush Pavilion philosophy — buildings conceived not to command a landscape, but to listen to it.

Framed entirely in Australian hardwoods and oriented to the northern sun, the residence gathers warmth through the cooler months and breathes with the valley through summer. Deep verandahs wrap the pavilion, softening the threshold between timber floor and forest floor, while high windows and louvres let the air move as it always has across this ridge.

The result is less a house than a quiet act of placemaking — a home that belongs to its setting as completely as the flooded gums and the creek below. This is architecture to be collected, and to be lived in, in equal measure.
